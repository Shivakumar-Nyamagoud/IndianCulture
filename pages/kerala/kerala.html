About Kerala

Kerala is nestled in the southwest part of India along the Malabar Coast. On November 1, 1956, the States Reorganisation Act led to the formation of this beautiful state which combines numerous regions speaking the common language called Malayalam.

Kerala is a treasure trove of India's rich heritage and vibrant culture. Kerala, often referred to as "God's Own Country," is a state located on the southwestern coast of India. It is renowned for its natural beauty, rich cultural heritage, and unique traditions.

The cultural landscape of Kerala is a blend of ancient traditions and historical influences. It boasts a diverse array of art forms, including classical music and dance such as Kathakali. The state's festivals are celebrated with great enthusiasm, with events like onam, visu showcasing the rich cultural fabric of the region.

Kerala cuisine is known for its use of coconut, rice, and spices. Popular dishes include Sadya (a traditional vegetarian feast), Appam, Puttu, and Kerala fish curry.

Kerala is home to classical art forms like Kathakali (a classical dance-drama known for its elaborate costumes and makeup), Mohiniyattam, and Kalaripayattu (one of the oldest martial arts in the world)..

Kerala’s backwaters, a network of canals and lagoons, are a major tourist attraction. Houseboat cruises in places like Alappuzha and Kumarakom offer a serene experience.

Hill Stations: Munnar, Wayanad, and Thekkady are known for tea plantations, cool climate, and scenic beauty.

Beaches: Kovalam and Varkala are popular beaches, attracting both domestic and international tourists.

Ayurveda: Kerala is famous for its Ayurvedic treatments, which promote holistic wellness through natural therapies.

The long-standing trade brought Kerala into contact with different cultures and religions. Early Jewish, Christian, and Muslim communities were established in Kerala through these trade routes, creating a vibrant cosmopolitan society

Whether it's exploring ancient ruins, experiencing traditional festivals, or savoring regional delicacies,kerala is a treasure trove of experiences that reflect the vibrant essence of India's heritage.

Heritage and kerala

Kerala is a land where multiple religions coexist in harmony, with influences from Hinduism, Christianity, Islam, and even ancient practices of Jainism and Buddhism

Traditional Kerala homes, known as Nalukettu, have sloping roofs and central courtyards to allow air circulation and natural cooling, essential for the region’s humid climate. Wood is used extensively, and many houses are adorned with intricate carvings

The state’s cultural and historical legacy continues to influence its modern identity, making it a vibrant and essential part of India's cultural mosaic.

Historical Heritage of kerala

It boasts a rich history, with significant contributions from ancient empires such as the Maurya, Gupta, and Mughal dynasties. Key historical sites include:

Uttar Pradesh, a state rich in historical significance, is home to some of India's most renowned heritage sites. From the iconic Taj Mahal and the majestic Fatehpur Sikri to the ancient city of Varanasi and the revered temples of Ayodhya, Uttar Pradesh offers a glimpse into India's diverse and illustrious past. These landmarks reflect the state's central role in shaping India's history, culture, and architecture, making it a vital repository of India's rich historical heritage.

Padmanabhaswamy Temple .

One of the 108 Divya Desams, Sree Padmanabhaswamy Temple is located in Thiruvananthapuram, the capital city of Kerala.

Embellished in gold-plated covering, the temple is opened only to Hindu followers. Dedicated to Lord Padmanabha; one of the avatars of Lord Vishnu, the Padmanabhaswamy Temple is one of the principal centres of Vaishnava worship in the dharma of Vaishnavism.

Fort Kochi

Fort Kochi, a historic region in the city of Kochi, Kerala, boasts a potpourri of European colonial influences that blend seamlessly with native traditions.

Often described as the 'Gateway to Kerala', its streets are lined with Portuguese, Dutch, and British colonial buildings, ancient churches, and quaint cafes.

The iconic Chinese fishing nets, reminiscent of ancient trading connections, adorn its shoreline, offering a picturesque view during sunse.

Kerala Folklore Museum .

Located in the heart of Kochi, the Kerala Folklore Museum is a treasure trove of Kerala's rich cultural, artistic, and architectural heritage.

Established to preserve and showcase the vibrant folklore and traditions of the region, the museum spans three floors, each dedicated to different historical periods: Malabar, Kochi, and Travancore..

Thrissur -The Cultural Capital of Kerala.

Thrissur is also known as the Cultural Capital of Kerala because of its cultural, spiritual, and religious leanings throughout history.

Thrissur had witnessed the rise and fall of various dynasties that include the Chera and Kulasekharas in the 12th century, Zamorins in the 14th and 15th century followed by Tipu Sultan, Portuguese, Dutch and British. In the 18th century, Raja Rama Varma, popularly known as Sakthan Thampuran, re-built Thrissur after attacks from Tipu Sultan.

Thrissur is also the hometown of the great Hindu Saint, Adi Shankara.

The city of Thrissur is home to several atractions such as temples, churches, and wildlife sanctuaries. The Vadakkumnathan temple, Thiruvambadi Sri Krishna Temple, Paramekkavu temple, Guruvayur Temple, Our Lady of Lourdes Syro-Malabar Catholic Metropolitan Cathedral, Our Lady of Dolours Syro-Malabar Catholic Basilica, Athirapally Falls, Elephant Sanctuary near Guruvayur, Peechi Dam & Sanctuary, Chimmony Wildlife Sanctuary, Chettuva Backwaters, Thrissur Zoo, Vilangankunnu, and Shakthan Thampuran Palace, and Kerala Kalamandalam are some important places to visit in Thrissur.

Thrissur hosts the world-famous Thrissur Pooram festival in April / May. This festival is the most colorful and spectacular temple festival in Kerala held at the Thekkinkadu Maidan. It is a festival that continues non-stop for 36 hrs with over 100 elephants, accompanied by the Panchavadyam playing till the final fireworks mark the end of the festival. UNESCO has awarded Thrissur Pooram as the most spectacular festival event on the planet. Thrissur is also known for its Onam celebrations in August / September every year.

Art & Handicrafts of Kerala

The state is known for its traditional arts and crafts, such as:

Aranmula Kannadi (Metal Mirrors):

The Aranmula Kannadi is a unique metal-alloy mirror produced only in the village of Aranmula in Kerala. Unlike traditional glass mirrors, these metal mirrors are highly polished to create a reflection. They are considered auspicious and are often used in rituals.

Nettur Petti (Traditional Jewelry Boxes):

Nettur Petti is a traditional handcrafted wooden jewelry box, usually made from rosewood. It has a characteristic pyramidal lid and is adorned with brass decorations.

Kasavu Sarees:

Kasavu sarees are traditional handwoven sarees that are synonymous with Kerala’s cultural identity. They are characterized by their off-white color and gold or silver borders. Worn during festivals like Onam and Vishu, as well as during weddings and temple visits, these sarees symbolize purity and elegance..

Mural Paintings:

Kerala’s mural tradition dates back to the 8th century and is characterized by vivid natural colors made from minerals, leaves, and flowers. The murals feature gods, goddesses, and scenes from epics like the Ramayana and Mahabharata.Famous examples of Kerala murals can be found in the Padmanabhapuram Palace, the Ettumanoor Shiva Temple, and the Mattancherry Palace.

Festivals of kerala

Kerala is a land of vibrant festivals that reflect its rich cultural heritage and diverse religious tradition. Major festivals include:

Kerala is a vibrant state known for its rich cultural and religious diversity, reflected in its wide array of festivals celebrated throughout the year. Each festival has its own unique significance and style of celebration, bringing people together in joy and devotion..

Onam - celebrated with grandeur across the state.

Onam is the most significant festival in Kerala, celebrated with great enthusiasm across the state. It marks the homecoming of the legendary King Mahabali, under whose reign Kerala experienced a golden age of prosperity. Onam is primarily a harvest festival and is celebrated with traditional feasts known as Onam Sadya, intricate flower designs called Pookalam, and the famous Vallam Kali (snake boat races). The festival also includes traditional dances like Thiruvathira and Pulikali, where performers paint themselves as tigers and hunters, adding color and excitement to the celebrations.

Vishu-

Vishu, celebrated in April, marks the Malayalam New Year and symbolizes prosperity. The highlight of Vishu is the Vishukkani, a special arrangement of fruits, flowers, rice, and a mirror, which is viewed first thing in the morning for good luck. Elders give money, known as Vishu Kaineettam, to children as blessings, and the day is filled with fireworks, traditional feasts, and temple visits, making it a joyful and auspicious occasion.

Thrissur Pooram- is one of Kerala’s most spectacular temple festivals

held annually at the Vadakkunnathan Temple in Thrissur. It features grand elephant processions, where richly adorned elephants parade through the streets, accompanied by traditional drum performances known as Chenda Melam. The highlight of the festival is the Kudamattam, a competition where teams swap colorful umbrellas atop the elephants. The event is capped off with a breathtaking fireworks display, making it a must-see for festival-goers..

Traditional Cuisine of Kerala

Sadya

One of the most iconic meals of Kerala is the Sadya, a traditional vegetarian feast served on a banana leaf during festivals like Onam and Vishu. Sadya includes a variety of dishes such as:

Sambar (lentil-based vegetable curry)

Avial (a mixed vegetable curry with coconut and yogurt)

Thoran (stir-fried vegetables with grated coconut)

Olan (a mild curry of ash gourd and black-eyed peas in coconut milk)

Pachadi (a yogurt-based dish with vegetables or fruits)

Payasam (a sweet pudding made from rice or lentils)

Uttar Pradesh offers a variety of mouth-watering dishes, influenced by its Mughal, Awadhi, and regional cuisines. Popular dishes include:

Rice and Tapioca: Matta rice, are often paired with curries. Tapioca, locally called Kappa, is another staple often served with spicy fish curry..

Puttu and Kadala Curry: Puttu is a cylindrical steamed rice cake made with rice flour and grated coconut, often served with Kadala Curry, a spicy black chickpea curry. This is a popular breakfast combination in Kerala..

Fish and Seafood:Being a coastal state, Kerala’s cuisine is famous for its seafood. Fish, prawns, crabs, and mussels are central to Kerala's diet.
